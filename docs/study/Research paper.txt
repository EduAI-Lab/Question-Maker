AI-Assisted Assessment Management for Equivalent Exam Variants






Abstract






The goal of this work is to determine whether an AI-assisted assessment management system can facilitate the construction of equivalent 
exam variants through structured question metadata and automated variant generation. Question Maker is a web-based system that embodies 
that design: persistent structured metadata, controlled automated variant generation from base 
questions, course-scoped retrieval-augmented generation, and mandatory instructor review before storage and export. We describe the 
architecture and report a structured evaluation in which an AP Computer Science A exam was the reference and two full variants were 
generated independently. Using an LLM-as-judge rubric over aligned slots, the composite exam scores were 86/100 and 85/100; rubric-only 
base scores were 98/100 for both. Every item was usable as-is or with minor edits, and no item was unusable; the main score compression 
came from moderate distinctness and usability factors, aligning with review-before-deployment as a structural safeguard. The evidence supports a qualified positive answer for text-based 
items on one reference exam; broader generalization is left to future work.






1  Introduction






Instructors who create assessments face recurring demands: designing new questions, managing question variants across offerings, and ensuring alignment with course content and learning objectives. General-purpose LLM tools can assist with question generation but leave instructors to manage storage, tracking, and review manually. This fragmented workflow makes it difficult to maintain consistent topical coverage, preserve relationships between question variants, or ensure that AI-generated content receives instructor review before deployment.








Question Maker addresses these limitations through a structured, instructor-mediated assessment authoring workflow. 
The system integrates AI-assisted generation, centralized question management, and instructor oversight within a single interface.
Instructors retain control over all content decisions: AI-generated text is not written to the question bank until explicitly approved,
and an assessment cannot be exported while any included item is still in draft. Review is mandatory in the 
software, not only by convention.








The research question we pursue is whether such a system can facilitate equivalent exam variants when variant construction is driven by structured metadata and automated generation rather than ad hoc prompting alone. Here equivalent means parallel-style forms that preserve construct coverage, difficulty, and topical alignment under the rubric in Section 6, not a claim of formal equating. Sections 3–4 describe the ingestion, variant-generation, assembly, and LLM-judge pipeline that Section 6 exercises; Section 4 only briefly notes other product capabilities outside that scope.

This paper describes the system's design, the workflow constraints it enforces, and the author-conducted evaluation summarized above.






2  Related Work




2.1  Assessment Platforms






Existing platforms such as PrairieLearn [9], Web-CAT [3], CodeWorkout [2], and Gradescope [8] support structured assessment management and automated grading.
These systems demonstrate the value of structured question representations and metadata when instructors deploy questions in live courses. Question Maker builds 
on this foundation by integrating AI-assisted generation directly into the assessment authoring workflow.






2.2  AI-Assisted Question Generation






Research on automated question generation has explored neural and conditional generative approaches [4, 7]. CS50's deployment of generative AI in education [5] demonstrates practical integration at scale. Question Maker differs by treating generation as a structured workflow step rather than a standalone capability, enforcing review before any generated content is stored.






2.3  Metadata and Variant Tracking






Maintaining structured metadata about question variants is central to repeatable assessment construction and to building alternate forms whose content can be traced and compared. Platforms and item banks that record topics, difficulty, and item families support instructors who must assemble interchangeable exams across sections or offerings. Question Maker links each variant row to one base question metadata record and stores difficulty, reasoning level, and topic identifiers. Section 3.1 summarizes that representation as used in the experiment.






2.4  Human-in-the-Loop Design






Human-in-the-loop design keeps human judgment over automated outputs to catch errors, maintain accountability, and calibrate trust [6]. Memarian and Doleck [6] note that many educational AI prototypes acknowledge human oversight, but it is often optional or informal rather than enforced by the tool. Question Maker requires explicit instructor approval before AI-generated content is saved to the bank and before assessments containing any draft item are exported.






3  System Overview

This section covers system features related to the experiment: ingesting a document of questions into the question bank, generating question variants with an external AI service that can apply course-scoped retrieval, assembling new full exams from the bank, and scoring baseline versus variant exams with an LLM judge. Other UI features are deferred to Section 4.







3.1  Course scope, bank structure, and metadata

Question Maker is organized by course. Each course has its own question bank; data from one course is not visible in another.

One logical exam item is modeled in two parts. A metadata record states what the item is: question type, primary topic, and other fields used for AI prompts and 
bookkeeping. One or more variant rows state how that item is worded on an exam. Each variant stores the question text that students read. For a multiple-choice item, 
that text is only the lead-in before the labeled options; the A/B/C/D lines are stored separately as the choice list. The variant also stores the correct answer, 
difficulty, reasoning level, draft or approved status, and a link to its metadata record. The first variant for that item is usually the baseline wording; further 
variants are alternates for the same construct.

Topics are instructor-defined labels with stable numeric IDs. Difficulty is easy, medium, or hard. Reasoning level is factual, analytical, or application. We use these 
terms consistently in Section 6.

Export and draft rules from Section 1 still apply for operational use, but the experiment analysis turns on bank content and assembled assessments rather than 
LMS export.







3.2  OCR and AI-assisted extraction into the bank

Instructors upload a document, which is parsed for text — via the browser's PDF text layer, Tesseract.js OCR for scanned pages, or direct reading for text files. The extracted text is sent to the backend, split into question-sized chunks, and passed to an AI endpoint along with the course topic list so each returned question can be tagged with a topic.

The service returns structured draft questions for instructor review before anything is saved. Non-textual figures and diagrams are not reconstructed, so items that 
depend on a picture may be missing or incomplete after extraction. Section 6 therefore reports results on text-only slots.

3.3  Automated variant generation and course-scoped RAG

The variant workflow targets one metadata record at a time. The implementation promotes the first stored variant for that record to non-draft status, then requests 
one or more new variants from the same AI backend used elsewhere in the app.

The user-visible prompt asks for a single alternate that preserves the learning objective and approximate difficulty while changing surface details so the item is 
not identical to the source. The original question text and, for multiple-choice items, the option count and reference option list are included so the model must return the 
same number of choices with new distractors. The course topic list is appended so primary and secondary topic IDs in the JSON output line up with the course taxonomy.

The backend call passes the course code into the remote generation API. That service performs retrieval-augmented generation: it can inject relevant chunks from the 
course’s uploaded materials into the model context alongside the explicit prompt. Difficulty and reasoning distributions sent to the API are set to match the baseline
 variant difficulty and reasoning level so the generator’s formatting constraints align with the slot being varied. Optional short free-text instructions from the 
 instructor are concatenated to the prompt when provided.

Successful responses are parsed as structured JSON, normalized, and persisted as new variant rows linked to the same metadata as the baseline. The Section 6 study
 ran this generation once per reference slot for each of two independent variant exams.







3.4  Assembling parallel exams from the bank

A reference assessment is an ordered list of section placements, each pointing to a specific variant of some metadata record. The assembly routine walks that order slot
 by slot. For every slot it needs another variant belonging to the same metadata record. It prefers a variant ID different from the one used on the reference row so the
  parallel exam does not repeat the exact wording when an alternate exists. The algorithm tracks which variant IDs have already been consumed so that two parallel exams
   built in one batch do not both pull the same alternate where distinct wording is desired.

The implementation creates new assessment records, a section shell, and ordered links from the section to the chosen variant rows, mirroring the reference length and
 order. The workflow UI can require a minimum count of non-draft variants per base item before treating a slot as ready for parallel assembly; that guard matches the
  need for at least one alternate in addition to the baseline.

3.5  LLM-as-judge rubric scoring

For evaluation, two assessments are compared: the reference baseline and a candidate variant exam built as in Section 3.4. The scorer loads both assessments’ variants 
in display order and pairs them by position.

For each pair, the system sends a chat-style request to the same AI backend with a system instruction that forbids solving the items or naming a multiple-choice letter.
 The user message contains the question text from both items, both answers, both choice arrays when present, and the rubric text. The model must return a single JSON
  object with ordinal scores for conceptual equivalence, difficulty similarity, structural validity, answer correctness, topic alignment, and distinctness, plus a 
  usability label and a one-sentence rationale. If parsing fails, the client retries once with stricter formatting instructions.

Aggregates over slots, weighted composites, usability penalties, and exam-level summaries are computed in application code. This judge is independent of any separate 
string-similarity checks; the study does not use a Jaccard or n-gram overlap stage for scoring.







4  Other capabilities

Question Maker also supports manual authoring, LMS integration, legacy imports, and additional assembly helpers. Those features support routine teaching workflows but 
are not part of the variant-equivalence experiment in Section 6 and are not discussed further here.





5  Limitations






First, this paper does not evaluate student learning outcomes, classroom deployment, or psychometric validation with live examinees. Section 6 assesses whether 
machine-generated variants approximate equivalence to a reference exam along rubric dimensions using an LLM judge. That judge is a proxy for instructor judgment, not a 
substitute for operational test-equating or outcome studies. The current implementation also depends on the external project's RAG infrastructure, which may influence 
behavior and generalizability. Further user testing across different courses and disciplines is needed to evaluate usefulness, robustness, and adoption.


Second, beyond the Section 6 reference-exam study, broader system use has been limited to a small set of computer science courses. Additional reference exams and 
disciplines are necessary to assess how well variant equivalence holds outside this setting.


Third, although the system enforces instructor review and approval, we do not quantify how review affects effort, trust, or long-term adoption.

Fourth, we do not yet report wall-clock human time spent correcting generated output, the number of regeneration attempts per item, or a controlled comparison against 
writing comparable items from scratch without the tool. Section 6.4 lists further evaluation protocol gaps for the AP CS A study.

Fifth, the current OCR-based ingestion does not capture non-textual elements such as diagrams or figures. As a result, questions that depend on visual context are 
incompletely represented in the input, leading to invalid or underspecified variants. For example, a generated question such as “Which classes can directly access the 
instance variable x?” may reference a class diagram present in the original exam but omitted during OCR, resulting in a variant that lacks the necessary context to be 
solvable. Consequently, the reported results apply only to text-based questions and do not reflect system performance on diagram-dependent items. These cases were 
typically identified through lower structural validity or answer correctness scores and classified as requiring edits. Addressing diagram-aware ingestion 
(e.g., multimodal OCR or structured figure parsing) remains an area for future work.




6  Initial Validation




This section operationalizes the paper's central question: whether an AI-assisted assessment management system can facilitate equivalent exam variants when production 
follows the pipeline in Sections 3.1–3.5, with other product features set aside as in Section 4. We use an AP Computer Science A (AP CS A) exam as the reference form. 
Full-exam variants are scored under an LLM-as-judge rubric on conceptual equivalence, difficulty similarity, structural clarity, answer correctness, and topic alignment,
with per-question usability labels. Equivalence here means rubric alignment judged by the model, not formal psychometric interchangeability.


All evaluation scenarios were conducted by the authors. The AP CS A exam was ingested into the system via OCR and used as the reference. Variants were generated with the
automated bank workflow in Section 3.3 for each slot, then assembled into full exams as in Section 3.4.


6.1  Evaluation Design


The reference exam comprised 47 questions spanning AP CS A topics including Java semantics, recursion, object-oriented design, sorting algorithms, and data structures.
 Using Question Maker, two full exam variants were generated independently, each constrained to the same primary topics as the reference exam. Each slot was generated
  from its ingested base question with metadata and variant linkage as described in Section 3.1, for rubric comparison to the reference.


We evaluated each generated exam variant against the reference exam using an LLM-as-judge rubric, implemented with gpt-oss:120b via Ollama, following the slot pairing
and JSON rubric protocol in Section 3.5, applied to every aligned question slot. We treat the LLM judge as an approximate proxy for instructor evaluation rather than 
a ground-truth authority. The judge assigned ordinal scores from 1 to 5 on five core dimensions: conceptual equivalence, difficulty similarity, structural validity, 
answer correctness, and topic alignment. We combined these into a fixed per-slot weighted composite with conceptual equivalence weighted 0.24 and each other dimension
0.19, averaged the composites across the exam, and linearly mapped the result from the 1–5 scale to a 0–100 rubric score for reporting. A separate per-slot
distinctness rating on the same 1–5 scale is converted to a multiplicative penalty factor and averaged across slots to down-weight variants that are too similar
to the original. An average usability factor from per-slot labels of usable as-is, usable with edits, or unusable further scales the score. The overall variant
score is the product of the 0–100 rubric score and these average factors, clamped to the interval from 0 to 100. 


Since the OCR pipeline does not preserve non-textual elements such as diagrams, questions requiring visual context were excluded from the evaluation. The experiment therefore focuses on text-based questions only.


6.1.1 Rubric
Each generated question variant was evaluated using a structured rubric across six dimensions: conceptual equivalence, difficulty similarity, structural validity, answer correctness, topic alignment, and variant distinctness. Each dimension was scored on a 1–5 ordinal scale, where higher values indicate stronger alignment with the reference question.
For example, a score of 5 in conceptual equivalence indicates that the variant assesses the same concept and reasoning process as the original, while a score of 1 indicates a completely different concept.
The full rubric definitions and scoring guidelines are provided in Appendix A.


6.1.2  Protocol scope and metrics reported

We produced two independent full-exam variant passes (Variant A and Variant B) from the same 47-slot reference exam, for a total of 94 generated slots scored by the judge. We report aggregate and per-variant rubric means, composite exam scores, per-item usability under the judge’s classification scheme from the evaluation design, and qualitative examples in Section 6.3. We did not log per-slot regeneration counts, model temperature or seed metadata for each click, or instructor editing time after generation; nor did we run a parallel condition in which authors authored matched items without Question Maker. Those measurements would directly address reliability and cost questions raised in review and are prioritized for future work.


6.2 Results


Variant A, corresponding to the exported judge report for AP CS A — Exam Variant 1, received a final exam variant score of 86/100 and a rubric-only base score of 98/100. Variant B, from AP CS A — Exam Variant 2, received 85/100 final and 98/100 rubric-only. In both runs all 47 slots were classified as usable as-is or usable with edits; none were unusable.




For Variant A, 43 of 47 questions (91%) were usable as-is and 4 (9%) usable with edits. Mean scores were: conceptual equivalence 4.96, difficulty similarity 4.94, structural validity 5.00, answer correctness 4.77, and topic alignment 4.98. Mean distinctness was 4.09/5 (factor 0.88).




For Variant B, 44 of 47 questions (94%) were usable as-is and 3 (6%) usable with edits. Mean scores were: conceptual equivalence 4.96, difficulty similarity 4.91, structural validity 4.96, answer correctness 4.77, and topic alignment 4.98. Mean distinctness was 4.02/5 (factor 0.87).




Across both variant exams, conceptual equivalence averaged 4.96 and topic alignment 4.98; difficulty similarity averaged 4.94 and 4.91; structural validity averaged 5.00 and 4.96. Answer correctness averaged 4.77 on both, the lowest dimension in these runs. Mean distinctness near 4.0 produced the multiplicative penalties in the composite score even when rubric-only bases stayed at 98/100.








6.3 Representative Cases






The following cases illustrate the range of outcomes observed in the aggregate results above, drawn from both ends of the composite score distribution.




Highest-scoring slots illustrate strong alignment with the reference. In Variant A, the judge gave composite 5.00/5 to several slots including slot 1 (Java pass-by-value with clear wording and distinct numbers), slot 4 (selection-sort intermediate state with new numbers), and slot 5 (array manipulation with comparable difficulty and a correct key). In Variant B, composite 5.00/5 appeared on slots such as 4 (again selection-sort intermediate state with new numbers), 6 (multi-level indexing and method-call reasoning), and 7 (nested-loop condition checking recast with a locker scenario). These cases show the system can produce items that match the baseline concept and structure while changing surface detail. A figure for this section is forthcoming.




Lower composite scores in these reports were tied to weak distinctness, not unusability. In Variant A, slot 43 scored 2.00/5 on the composite while still labeled usable as-is: the judge described an array-order check that changed only names and stayed very close to the baseline wording. In Variant B, slot 38 scored 2.00/5 while usable as-is: the same reliability concept with nearly identical options and minor rewording. Separately, a few slots in each exam were usable with edits because of answer-key or clarity issues; for example, in Variant B the judge flagged slot 5 as usable with edits after an incorrect marked answer on an array-loop variant. Those items illustrate where human review still matters before deployment.






6.4  Limitations of the Evaluation




Limitations bound the interpretation of these results. First, the LLM judge assesses validity along rubric dimensions but cannot substitute for instructor 
subject-matter judgment, particularly for correctness of answer keys in domain-specific questions. Scores on the answer correctness dimension should be treated as a 
triage signal for which questions most likely need review, not as a definitive correctness verdict.




Second, this evaluation used a single reference exam from one course and discipline. Whether these validity preservation rates generalize to other subjects, question types, or difficulty levels is an open question. The system's RAG-based generation is grounded in uploaded course materials, so performance is expected to vary with the quality and breadth of those materials.




Third, the distinctness dimension rewards surface-level variation but does not capture deeper forms of exam security such as underlying structure or similar answer choices. This is reflected in mean distinctness near 4.0–4.1 in these runs, which indicates only moderate surface variation and leaves room for deeper structural diversity. An exam that scores well on distinctness may still be recognizable to a student who has seen the original. Deeper distinctness analysis remains future work.

Fourth, the high rubric scores indicate strong preservation of assessment properties; however, the relatively modest distinctness penalty highlights that many variants remain structurally similar to the reference exam.

Fifth, we did not independently audit whether every saved variant strictly matched its selected reasoning level; conceptual equivalence and difficulty similarity in 
the rubric may absorb some misalignment, but Bloom-style tag compliance was not scored on its own.







6.5  Analysis






The results support that Question Maker preserves properties that matter for parallel-form style assessment. Mean conceptual equivalence was 4.96 on both variant exams, mean topic alignment 4.98, mean difficulty similarity 4.94 and 4.91, and mean structural validity 5.00 and 4.96. These patterns align with the sort of validity argument educators make when claiming alternate forms target the same constructs at similar demand [1]. Together with 47 slots per variant and the usability counts in Section 6.2, they support the claim that the system can facilitate variant construction at exam scale, with instructor review still needed for occasional answer-key fixes and for items with low distinctness.






The judge’s mean answer-correctness score of 4.77 and the small number of “usable with edits” slots point to occasional key or clarity fixes rather than wholesale failure. Items remain editable and unexported until approved, so those fixes are caught before students see them. Low composite scores on individual slots were often driven by distinctness penalties when wording stayed close to the baseline, not by unusability.






7  Conclusion and Future Work

We asked whether an AI-assisted assessment management system can facilitate equivalent exam variants through structured question metadata and automated variant 
generation. On a single AP CS A reference exam, two independently generated full variants achieved composite scores of 86/100 and 85/100 under an LLM judge, with rubric-only bases of 98/100 each and no unusable slots. We answer the question in the affirmative for this setting, subject to the limitations in Section 6.4.

A direct comparison against unconstrained LLM generation was not included; such a baseline would help isolate the value of structured metadata and workflow. We leave
that comparison, diagram-capable pipelines, more reference exams, live instructor studies, and instrumented measurements of editing time, regeneration counts, and cost
relative to authoring from scratch to future work. The reported results apply only to text-based questions and not to diagram-dependent items.
